About Us

Our project represents the collaborative efforts of four data science students from Monash University, all in their third year of study. The primary goal of our project is to develop a robust forecasting model capable of predicting electricity load for the upcoming 48 hours. This task became particularly critical in the wake of the COVID-19 pandemic, as electricity load demands underwent significant fluctuations that traditional models struggled to accurately capture. As a response, our project aims not only to build a prediction model that adapts effectively to such dynamic changes but also to integrate an automated retraining mechanism to ensure ongoing accuracy.

To achieve this objective, we explored three different modeling approaches: XGBoost, Random Forest, and Linear Regression. These models were rigorously tested against three benchmark models: Naive, Seasonal Naive (with varying seasonalities). Our findings revealed that all models outperformed the benchmarks, demonstrating their effectiveness. Among these models, XGBoost emerged as the preferred choice due to its consistent error measures, which closely matched those of Random Forest, while offering faster and more efficient performance.

Prior to commencing the modeling phase, extensive data preprocessing was carried out. This included thorough data cleaning, which involved addressing null values, handling outliers, ensuring accurate data types, and more. These preprocessing steps are also applied when users submit files to the system, ensuring data integrity throughout.

Moreover, Our project incorporates an automated retraining framework designed to monitor and address data drift, ensuring that the model remains accurate over time. The selection of models and retraining frameworks was guided by a comprehensive literature review, drawing insights from similar cases in the field.

Our implementation of the model is designed to be multi-step, leveraging 48 instances of the XGBoost model to predict load for each hour within the 48-hour forecast window.

The heart of our project lies in the 'Forecaster' page, where users can submit two files, each containing 24 hours of load and weather data for the next two days, or a single file encompassing all 48 hours. The intuitive user interface generates prediction results in the form of informative graphs, plotting the forecasted load.

Users also have the option to upload 'actual' load values and real weather data from the preceding two days. The system then generates a comparison graph showcasing the actual versus predicted values, along with essential error metrics such as Mean Absolute Error (MAE), Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE), and Root Mean Square Error (RMSE).

For a deeper historical perspective, the 'Historic Performance' page provides a comprehensive overview of all predictions made by the model to date. It displays associated error metrics, offering insights into the model's evolving performance.

Lastly, the 'Insights' page features a collection of graphs that delve into the dataset. These visualizations explore seasonality patterns and uncover correlations between variables, providing valuable insights into the nature of the data.

Our project represents a holistic approach to electricity demand forecasting, addressing the challenges posed by dynamic load patterns and incorporating automation for long-term accuracy.
